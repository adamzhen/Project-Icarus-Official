Alfvén (Elf Van!) surface

VERY BIG. The corona stretches up to about 13 million km (18.8 solar radii) above the sun's surface. That means you could fit another 18 suns across just the diameter of the corona! The edge of the corona is called the Alfvén surface, represented here by a jolly elf van zipping around the sun. At the Alfvén surface, the sun's gravitational and magnetic fields become too weak to hold in plasma particles (which, by the way, the sun is constantly spitting out), so they fly out into space as solar wind.


That's the same solar wind that can produce stunning auroras here on Earth.

But it's also the same solar wind that, if strong enough, can damage not only satellites and astronauts in space, but also electrical grids and telecommunication networks on Earth. Another level above solar winds are solar storms, where massive amounts of plasma are ejected from a region of the corona - fittingly, called coronal mass ejections (CMEs). Typically, these CMEs happen anywhere from once every 5 days to once every 8 hours, and if one of them happens to hit Earth, they can have similar effects as solar wind, but way worse. The largest solar storm ever recorded, called the Carrington Event, was back in 1859, when the only electric systems were telegrams. But if it happened today, it would likely wipe out our entire power grid for weeks or even months on end. By getting up close and personal with the source of solar winds and solar storms, the Parker Solar Probe will help us better understand them and better prepare for potential disasters.
