Wait, so how big is the corona?

Alfvén (Elf Van!) surface

VERY BIG. The corona stretches up to about 13 million km (18.8 solar radii) above the sun's surface. That means you could fit another 18 suns across just the diameter of the corona! The edge of the corona is called the Alfvén surface, represented here by a jolly elf van zipping around the sun. At the Alfvén surface, the sun's gravitational and magnetic fields become too weak to hold in plasma particles (which, by the way, the sun is constantly spitting out), so they fly out into space as solar wind.


That's the same solar wind that can produce stunning auroras here on Earth!

But it's also the same solar wind that, if strong enough, can damage not only satellites and astronauts in space, but also electrical grids and telecommunication networks on Earth. Yet solar wind is just a blip on the radar compared to solar storms, where massive amounts of plasma are ejected at once from the corona - fittingly, called coronal mass ejections (CMEs).

Typically, these CMEs happen anywhere from once every 5 days to once every 8 hours, and if one of them happens to hit Earth, they can have the same types of effects as solar wind, but way worse. The largest solar storm ever recorded, called the Carrington Event, was back in 1859, when (thankfully) the only electrical systems were telegrams. But if it happened today, it would likely wipe out our power grid for months or even years on end!

By getting up close and personal with the source of these solar phenomena in the corona, the Parker Solar Probe will be able to use its instruments to get a better picture of them and help prepare for potential disasters.

What the WISPR (Wide-field Imager for Solar PRobe) essentially does is it uses its 2 telescope cameras to take never-before-seen images of the corona and plasma particles that other instruments will directly measure. The cameras are stored inside a wacky, shoebox-sized contraption, each behind a protective door that was closed during launch to keep them safe.


Now, if you've ever pointed a camera at the sun, you'll know that it just blows out everything else because it's so absurdly bright. This is why the WISPR points at an angle away from the sun - the inner camera with a 40° field of view starting 58.5° from the sun's center, and the outer camera with a 58° field of view ending about 160° from the sun's center. Beyond sheltering the WISPR from a blistering heat death, the heat shield also blocks most of the sun's light, allowing the WISPR to effectively capture the much fainter corona.
